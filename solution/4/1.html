Solution: Famous by Association

Written by Nathan Pinsker and Lucy Zhang

The puzzle consists of some flavor text, followed by 32 startup names on the second page and 20 "Like X, for Y!"-style startup "business models". The flavor text instructs solvers to match each startup with its short slogan, and the boss who tells you to ignore the startups who "just submitted a name" is meant to suggest that the extra twelve names are red herrings.

Like all trendy startups nowadays, these startups' names are all plays on words. Their names point to either their function or their target audience, sometimes both at once.

All mappings, accompanied by explanations, follow:

Like Tinder, for robots!Spark (Tinder is a dating site, meant to stoke the flames of love; "Spark" refers to electricity, and both tinder and sparks start fires)
Like Slack, for physicists!Ansible (Slack is used for communication, and an ansible is a fictional device used for faster-than-light communication)
Like Mechanical Turk, for zombies!Brain (Mechanical Turk is a service to employ humans to perform simple "human intelligence" tasks that require brainpower, and zombies hunger for brains)
Like Spotify, for animals!Howl (Spotify is a music service, and howling is an animalistic way of making music)
Like travelmob, for teenagers!Slumbr (travelmob is a home rental service; "Slumbr" sounds like "slumber", after "slumber parties" which would probably be the main use-case scenario of this service)
Like the blockchain, for food!Hash (blockchains rely on the concept of a "hash", and hash is a food dish made with meat and potatoes)
Like Zynga, for animals!Leapfrog (Zynga is a gaming company, and Leapfrog is a game based around an animal)
Like Square, for pirates!galle.on (Square is a financial services company; a galleon is a common pirate ship and is also strongly associated with gold due to the Harry Potter series)
Like OKCupid, for teenagers!Crush (OKCupid is a dating website, and crushes are how teenage love often manifests)
Like Houzz, for animals!Nest (Houzz is a house decoration website, and a nest is a home for animals)
Like a16z, for musicians!Pitch (a16z, or Andreessen Horowitz, is a venture capital firm; startups "pitch" their ideas to such firms in order to receive funding, and pitch is a core perceptual property of music)
Like Snapchat, for animals!Goldfish (Snapchat is a sharing service where pictures disappear after a short delay, and goldfishes colloquially have short memories)
Like BuzzFeed, for robots!Shockr (refers to electricity, and BuzzFeed's common "[...] will SHOCK you!"-style "clickbait" articles)
Like TaskRabbit, for wizards!House Elf (TaskRabbit is a website for hiring people to perform tasks for you; house elves do wizards' chores)
Like YCombinator, for ninjas!stealth.ly (YCombinator is a startup accelerator; startups that work with YCombinator are frequently in "stealth mode", and ninjas value stealth)
Like Groupon, for animals!Cheep (an animal sound that also sounds like "cheap"; Groupon is a coupon site)
Like Upworthy, for teenagers!Cliq (sounds like "clique", something associated with teenagers, as well as "click"; Upworthy is a "clickbait"-style site)
Like Sporcle, for mathematicians!trivi.al (Sporcle is a trivia site, and mathematicians commonly use the word "trivial")
Like Soylent, for robots!char.gr (Soylent is a staple food company; food restores energy, and robots restore their energy via chargers)
Like HelloSign, for artists!Form (HelloSign is an online form-signing site, and form is an element of artistic composition)

At this point, solvers will now have a way of uniquely associating each slogan with a startup, and hence with a monetary value, in millions. The last step is to put these two mappings together, using each monetary value to index into the corresponding slogan (but dividing them by one million first â€” startups sure do have lots of money to throw around!). Since the startups are provided in alphabetical order, this suggests that keeping the slogans in their given order is the correct way of ordering. Performing this step yields the phrase:

FRUIT OF QUORAS DANGELO

"Quora's D'Angelo" refers to Adam D'Angelo, the CEO of Quora, Inc. Thus the answer is ADAM'S APPLE.

Author's Notes

This puzzle started its life as "Hacker News vs. Snacker News", a parody of the 2017 Mystery Hunt puzzle Crackpot or Snackpot? (itself based on the site arXiv vs. snarXiv) but modeled off the popular tech news site Hacker News. We wanted to poke fun at startup culture, especially its fondness for snappy yet childish names, and its penchant for claims of "We're changing the world!" with nothing more than a web application.

Originally, the cluephrase for the answer at the end was LIKE APPLE FOR THROATS. While it fit better with the theme, it steered some testsolvers towards finding (or making up) a company, and away from the intended "don't think too hard about this"-style pun.
