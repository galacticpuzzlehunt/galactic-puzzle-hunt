Solution: A Basic Puzzle

Written by Brian Chen

There are a few ways to approach this puzzle: you might observe that the bolded number contains many contiguous sequences of repeated digits and think of a way to interpret it, or you might take inspiration from the puzzle title and try expressing the integers in different bases.

If you treat the bolded number 7777337833662777998 as a series of key presses on a telephone keypad, you will find that it spells SEPTENARXT. (For instance, 7777 = four 7’s = the fourth letter on the 7 key = S, 33 = two 3’s = the second letter on the 3 key = E, and so on.) The start of this is promising, but the ending is not quite right. However, if the number had been 1 more, namely 7777337833662777999, the letters would perfectly spell the word SEPTENARY, or base 7. This confirms the puzzle title that we’re supposed to interpret numbers in different bases.

If you try expressing the other numbers in base 7, you will find that 60643020435392382204634988156634119 in base 7 is 123450136010234501501235013450101235013441, notable for its beginning of “12345” and consisting mostly of strictly increasing sequences 1..6 separated by 0’s. What encoding system uses subsets of six elements? Braille! If you treat each of the segments between 0s as specifying a sequence of raised dots in Braille, you find that this encodes QUATERNAR? (where the last letter cannot be translated from 13441). If you had added 12 to the number, you would obtain a perfect encoding of QUATERNARY, or base 4.

Continuing in this way, you form a chain of numbers that, when incremented by a small number, expressed in a certain base, and then interpreted by some encoding, give names of other bases.

Author's Notes

An encoding that matched pairs of numbers from 1 to 5 to the alphabet, using the fact that 5×5 ≈ 26, would seem to be a natural candidate for inclusion in this puzzle, and was suggested during testsolving. However, there are at least three plausible ways to actually correspond pairs of 1..5 to letters of the alphabet: we could leave the Z off (similar to the lexicographical permutations), combine C and K as in the tap code, or combine I and J as in the Polybius square. Due to the ambiguity, I didn’t include any such encoding in this puzzle.

Although I personally like puzzlehunt encodings quite a bit, I think their overuse can be somewhat inelegant, so I was glad almost none of the other puzzles used any of these encodings. Then I could have no qualms about having stuffed all of them into this one puzzle. The encoding I was the most concerned about was the one using keyboard intersections — I knew it had featured in a SUMS puzzle a few years back, though, so I hoped that some solvers would think of it from there; I was also pleasantly surprised to note during the hunt that the puzzle's solution showed up in Google searches for "puzzlehunt encodings". I also tried to put the less standard encodings in positions where you could miss them and still be able to guess the final answer. On the other hand, the encoding into a 26-digit number was apparently harder than I expected.

I hope everybody enjoyed this puzzle, despite some of the more obscure encodings used.

During the hunt, a team pointed out to us that the base-21 representation of 1606929535271984790707306 contained the substring "BA5IC", an amazing unintentional red herring that unfortunately nobody noticed during testsolving. Apologies to any teams who were tripped up by this!
